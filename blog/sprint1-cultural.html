by Lory Costa

Identity, Values and Strenghts Exploration

05/02/2021

About my values

I grew up in Brazil, with my parents and four siblings. My father was self-employed, and things were always changing for him, so my family moved a lot from place to place throughout my life. That helped me develop one of my most vital traits—adaptability. We didn't have many material things, but we were close and happy most of the time.

When I was eleven years old, my family joined the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints, that changed our lives for good. With the gospel of Jesus Christ's principles, I learned self-reliance and acquired a great desire for growth. That has shaped my identity and values more than anything else in my life. Our net of friends expanded dramatically. We now knew many people from different parts of the world. That got me interested in learning other languages, especially English, which I didn't realise at the time, but would positively influence my life in the future.

After that time, I discovered my love for books, nature, dancing, and learning and I became better at making friends and paying more attention to people's needs. That lead me to serve as a missionary for the church when I was twenty-one—a delightful accomplishment that taught me the value of compassion, discipline and hard work.

When I got back home from my mission, I decided to leave my parent's house and move to Florianópolis, in the south of Brazil, where I studied Human Resources Management and met my husband, Matt. While working at two jobs to afford rent and pay for my studies, I discovered how determined I could be and how much I valued independence.

I finished school two weeks before my wedding, and eighteen months later, we had a four-month-old baby and a plane ticket for Matt to come to New Zealand. Three months later, in August 2014, baby and I joined him, and together we made New Zealand our home. Leaving everything behind to have a chance at a better future for our kids was just the first of the sacrifices. We developed more faith and resilience and grew in our love for each other, as we journeyed on, with no family or friends' support while raising kids in a different country.

Life in New Zealand has been amazing to me. In 2015 I got a job as an HR coordinator, working with a fantastic team, met terrific people and learned a lot about the kiwi way of doing things. I fell in love with the culture from the beginning and did my best to be accepted and accepting.

After three years at the job, I decided to quit after I got back from maternity leave, I missed my baby too much, I was stressed, and I thought it was the right moment to focus on the kids. Then last year, after three years at home, I felt ready to go back to work, but thinking of HR didn't bring me joy, so I decided to look for something new, and here I am, at Dev Academy, showing my courage and optimism, hoping for a great future.

"The future is as bright as your faith". Thomas S. Monson

About my strengths and limitations

When contemplating what to do next, I retook a test I had taken years ago, the 16 personalities test. It turns out I haven't changed in all those years; I am still an INFJ or Advocate. Some of my strengths, according to the test, are:

  • Creative
  • Insightful
  • Principled
  • Passionate
  • Altruistic

    • And my weaknesses:

  • Sensitive to Criticism
  • Reluctant to Open Up
  • Perfectionistic
  • Avoiding the Ordinary
  • Prone to Burnout

    • I don't know to what extent I agree to all of those things, but I can see some of them in myself. I believe I am passionate and principled. When I commit to something or someone, I follow through, no matter what. And it is also true that I can be reluctant to open up, I tend to keep my problems and concerns to myself, and I am sensitive to some kinds of criticism, but marriage has helped me improve a lot in those areas. :)

    I think my determination and love of learning and feeling productive are a plus in terms of learning and career development. In contrast, my perfectionism can be an enemy in that area. Sometimes I have difficulty letting go, I want to keep working on the issue until it's perfect, but I know that that is not always the best approach, and I trust Dev Academy will have some lessons to teach me on that.

    I could not think of an ethical decision I had to make, but I have something to share on working productively with others when there was resistance or tension.

    In 2017, when I worked as an HR coordinator, my relationship with my manager became distant. When I went on maternity leave, the department hired two people to replace me, a clear indicator of how overloaded I was. When I got back, my manager became gradually more irritable and impatient with me. She expected me to resume all the activities that my two replacements had been working on, and I did, as best as possible, but I had a massive amount of work and very little flexibility. So I decided the best approach would be to have an honest conversation. I started by writing her a letter, stating everything that was on my mind. The next day, I rewrote it, removing anything that didn't pass through Socrates' three sieves (this is something I have been doing since I was fifteen, it's a principle for me), and finally, I made it into bullet points and scheduled a meeting with her. I started by asking for her feedback on how I was doing, and then I said what I had to say. I then discovered she had been sick for several months and was struggling. We both left the room, after tears and hugs, with a new-found appreciation for each other, and determined to be more explicit about our feelings and goals.

    It was a revealing and powerful experience for me. I learned that not everything is about me and that there has to be good communication for a team to work in harmony. If it were now, I would not wait until things got to that point, openness and empathy are fundamental to good teamwork.
